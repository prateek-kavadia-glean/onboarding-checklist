Why this matters now

Discovery with Jason and the support leaders aligned on three pressures: international growth, how support scales when product knowledge is concentrated, and order status visibility in NetSuite.

Growth

International expansion needs a different support model

MCWW is not looking to train every new hire from zero on car wash systems alone—you need answers in flow. Jason framed the goal as eventually supporting international markets (e.g. broader coverage / call-center models) after Tier 1 and Tier 2 can resolve more with better tools, not just more bodies.

Support

Tier 1 and Tier 2 need to carry more of the load

Today’s workflow runs through Zendesk, manuals, file storage, email, and Teams/Slack before Tier 3 and engineering. Jason described the support function as on the order of 18 people with three leads (Mike, Dan, Micah) bearing much of the load—so when Tier 1 cannot self-serve, experienced people stay underwater. Glean targets that gap first.

Ops visibility

Order status should not require a custom report every time

NetSuite is the direction for operational truth, but “ask the status of this order” has been hard to satisfy with static reports alone. A grounded Q&A layer—where connectors allow—reduces the email ping-pong Jason described on calls.

Business impact: Faster response to customers and distributors, less busywork for sales and ops, fewer mistakes from stale threads.

Why buy now · why not build · why start small

On the Mar 27 connect call, Jason described ownership’s starting point: a culture rooted in manufacturing (“manufacturer of metal”), where software is newer—and some leaders ask why pay for this when we could put an AI bot in our own data center. The answer for the business case is not a platform lecture; it is time-to-value, connectors, permissions, and who maintains them, starting with support and sales/ops where pain is already acute.

Why buy now

Support strain and international growth do not wait for a perfect data estate. A packaged product with enterprise connectors and security controls gets you to measured value in weeks, not a multi-year internal build.

Why not build it ourselves

“Build” still means maintaining connectors, permissions, models, and UX across Zendesk, Microsoft, NetSuite, Slack, and more—plus staff to keep them current. That is a product team, not a side project. Buy the outcome; do not underestimate the long-term cost of build.

Why start small (Devon’s direction)

Land minimum value first: a clear scope, a few systems, a defined team. Prove fewer escalations and faster answers. Expand once the story is simple and credible for the rest of the business—avoid boiling the ocean on day one.

Recommended starting scope

Make deployment scope and cost tradeoffs obvious: connect what is easy and high-value first; defer custom and migrating systems until after the first win.

Phase 2 / custom integration candidates

Important systems—but do not pay professional services to integrate these on day one unless there is a hard blocker:

  • Dataworks
  • WashLink / vBulletin
  • Other custom, legacy web, or on-premises sources
Defer heavy spend

NAS / on-prem file share: Support is already pushing to get content off local storage because tier-one reps lack access. Connecting NAS through Glean is on the order of ~40 engineering hours—so it belongs in the 50-hour implementation path only if day-one access is truly worth it. On the Mar 27 call, Jason argued it does not make business sense to pay for that integration when the end state is Microsoft cloud anyway—use the project as a forcing function to finish migration, then index where files land.
